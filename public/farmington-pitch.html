Why Farmington. Why Now.
The Case for Moving First
Four Converging Forces
1.  New Leadership, New Mandate

City Manager Shaña Reeves assumed leadership with a 22-year institutional track record and a clear drive to modernize Farmington's operations. She was handed two regulatory violation notices from day one. AIMS gives her the forward-looking data infrastructure to demonstrate proactive, next-generation governance from the start.

2.  Regulatory Pressure is Accelerating

Draft permit NM0031135 brings new monitoring requirements. Federal PFAS MCLs are in force. NMED has already cited Farmington twice this year. The question isn't whether enhanced monitoring is coming — it's whether Farmington gets ahead of it or reacts to it.

3.  The Drug Crisis Demands New Tools

108,400 fentanyl pills. 7 overdoses in county detention. 20 trafficking operations. San Juan County is at the epicenter of NM's fentanyl epidemic. Wastewater epidemiology is now endorsed by CDC, SAMHSA, and the White House ONDCP as a public health intelligence tool. Farmington can lead.

4.  Environmental Justice Obligation

The San Juan River is sacred and essential to Navajo Nation communities downstream. Documenting what Farmington's WWTP is and isn't removing — and actively reducing that burden — is not just regulatory compliance. It's the right thing to do, and it protects the city from future liability.
